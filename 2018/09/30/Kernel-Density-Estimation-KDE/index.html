Kernel Density Estimation (KDE)

Posted on 2018-09-30 | In Data Mining | Visitors:

What is Density Estimation (DE)?

  • Observe some data xi
  • Estimate p(x)

Why do DE?

  • Find unusual observations
  • Find typical observations
  • Classifier via Bayes Rule

Parametric vs. Nonparametric DE

Previously we assumed that at the very least the parametric form of the likelihoods p(x|θ) were known
flipping of a coin, rolling of a die, …

What if we don’t know the parametric form of likelihoods?

Non-parametric Density Estimation

Estimate the density directly from the data without assuming a particular form for the underlying distribution

Examples:

  • Histograms
  • Parzen windows
  • Smooth kernels

Histograms

Most common form of non-parametric density estimation

  • Split data range into equal-sized bins
  • For each bin, count the # of data points that fall into the bin
  • Y axis: frequency(e.g. counts for each bin)
  • X axis: values of the variable

The histogram can illustrate features related to the distribution of the data

  • Center(i.e.,thelocation)
  • Spread(i.e.,thescale)
  • Skewness
  • Presenceofoutliers
  • Presence of multiple modes

Histograms Parameters

Histogram requires two parameters to be defined:

  1. Bin width
  2. Starting position of bin

Issues with Histograms

  1. For small data sets, histograms can be misleading
  2. For large data sets, histograms can be quite effective at illustrating general properties of the distribution
  3. Histograms effectively only work with 1 variable at a time
  4. Very difficult to extend to more than 2 dimensions
  5. Histograms tell us nothing about the relationships among variables
  6. DE depends on the starting position of the bins
  7. Discontinuities of the estimate are not due to the underlying density
  8. Curse of dimensionality

Smoothing histograms

Can smooth histograms using a variety of techniques – e.g. KDE (short for Kernel Density Estimation)

Density Estimation

Given a set of n data samples x1,…, xn, we can estimate the density function p(x), so that we can output p(x) for any new sample x. This is called density estimation.

If k out of the n data samples fall in the region s we arrive at the following obvious R, then the estimate for p(x) is

$$ p(x) = \frac{k/n}{V} $$

General Expression for Non-Parametric Density Estimation

$$ p(x) \cong \frac{k}{NV} $$
Where:
V: volume surrounding x
N: total # of data samples
k: # data samples inside V

Parzen windows

Parzen Window Density Estimation

  • Suppose R is a hypercube centered at x
  • Let h be the length of the edge of the hypercube
  • $V=h^{2}$ for a square
  • $V=h^{3}$ for a cube
  • $V=h^{d}$ for a d-dimensional object

The window function: We want a function that indicates if y is inside the square centered at x with width h

$$ \phi(\frac{y-x}{h}) = 1,\ if \frac{|y_d - x_d|}{h} \le \frac{1}{2} for \ d = 1,2. \ (otherwise \ 0)$$

The total number k samples falling within the region R, out of n, is given by

$$ k = \sum_{i=1}^{n} \phi \frac{x-x_i}{h} $$
x: the new example
n: total # of examples
h: bandwidth

Smooth kernels

The Parzen window has several drawbacks

  • It yields density estimates that have discontinuities
  • It weights equally all points $x_i$, regardless of their distance to the estimation point 𝑥

For these reasons, the Parzen window is commonly replaced with a smooth kernel function K(x)

Bandwidth selection

The problem of choosing 𝒉 is crucial in density estimation

  • A large h will over-smooth the DE and mask the structure of the data
  • A small h will yield a DE that is spiky and very hard to interpret

A natural measure is the MSE (short for mean squared error) at the estimation point x

$$ MSE(p{KDE} (x)) =E[(p{KDE} (x)−p(x))^2] = bias^2+variance $$

Bias-variance trade-off

  • The bias of an estimate is the systematic error incurred in the estimation
  • The variance of an estimate is the random error incurred in the estimation

Rules of Thumb

  1. $p_{KDE}(x)$ is our estimate for p(x)
  2. Absolute value of bias = $ |E[p_{KDE}(x)] – p(x)| $
  3. As the bandwidth h increases, the bias of $ p{KDE} (x) $ increases and the variance of $ p{KDE} (x) $ decreases

Bandwidth Selection Methods

  1. Subjective choice
  • Plot out several curves and choose the estimate that best matches one’s prior (subjective) ideas
  • Not practical since we typically have high- dimensional data
  1. Objective choice
  • Maximum likelihood cross-validation
  • MLE of h is degenerate since it yields $ h_{MLE} $ = 0
  • Maximize the pseudo-likelihood computed using leave-one-out cross-validation
